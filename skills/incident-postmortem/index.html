Incident Post-mortem

An evidence-first method for writing an incident post-mortem. It verifies every factual claim against a durable artifact, forces a real 5 Whys instead of prose, and produces a record that still reads true a year later, not a transcript of the war room.

Get the skill (Markdown)

In one paragraph

The difference between a trustworthy post-mortem and a plausible-looking one is where its claims come from. Incident channels and call transcripts are full of live hypotheses people voiced while debugging, which are leads to verify, not facts to publish. This skill enforces one rule above all: every factual claim is backed by a durable artifact (code, a merged PR, a config console, a ticket, production data), and anything that cannot be fact-checked is cut, not softened. Around that core it standardizes the section structure, forces Root Cause to be a real 5 Whys, and strips the blame, approximate numbers, and meta-narration that quietly make reviewers distrust the whole document.

What it is

  • An evidence discipline: list every causal claim the transcript suggests, prove each against an artifact, keep only what you proved.
  • A canonical structure: Intro, Metadata, Summary, Impact, Root Cause, Action Items, Full COE Required?, and a recommended Evidence section that makes every claim auditable.
  • A hard rule that Root Cause is a real 5 Whys (question → because → question, down to the true root cause), with no side-sections.
  • A from-scratch build workflow for when an existing post-mortem needs to be redone from evidence rather than reworded.
  • Agent-agnostic: written for any skill-aware AI coding agent; adapt the template and publish steps to your own wiki.

What it does

  • Separates the trigger (the change that set it off) from the amplifier (what made it worse), and keeps upstream responsibility clear without blame.
  • Forces exact, verified numbers from logs or the database, reserving the approximate "~" for sourced external expectations only.
  • Requires every ticket, PR, and prior post-mortem to be a described link, never a bare ID.
  • Reads the existing doc's review comments as the must-fix list before rewriting.
  • Verifies after publishing: re-fetch the stored form, re-run the hygiene checks, confirm every section and link resolves.

What it does not do

  • It does not let a claim survive on the strength of "the war room thought so": unbacked claims are cut, not attributed.
  • It does not accept a paragraph that describes the cause in place of an actual 5 Whys.
  • It does not end a 5 Why at organizational fault. It ends at the technical mechanism.
  • It does not tolerate approximate measurements, bare-ID links, mixed timezones, or meta-narration about how the doc was written.
  • It does not publish secrets, local paths, or raw logs into a record meant to stay durable.

When to reach for it

Any time an incident has been mitigated and needs a written record: "write the post-mortem", "document this incident", or "the existing post-mortem is bad, redo it from scratch". Best used within the usual one-business-day window after mitigation, while the evidence is still fresh and the tickets are still open.

The discipline, in six rules

  1. Evidence over transcript: a claim is only as good as the artifact that proves it; cut what you cannot check.
  2. A real 5 Whys: chain symptom to root cause in question/because pairs, no side-sections.
  3. Exact numbers: measurements from logs or the database; "~" only on a sourced external estimate.
  4. Described links: every ticket, PR, and prior doc is a hyperlink with human-readable text, never a bare ID.
  5. No blame, no meta: attribute code ownership factually; never narrate how the doc was written.
  6. Verify after publish: re-fetch, re-scan, confirm every section and link, guard against version drift.
